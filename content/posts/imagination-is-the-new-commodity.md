`Build me a retro 80s game in the style of an arcade cabinet you would have found in a faded seaside cafe. Chunky pixels. Synthy colours. Slightly ridiculous enemies. Make it fast, playful, and just a little bit weird.`

A sentence like that would not have counted as serious software work for very long. It is not a spec. It is not a ticket. It is barely even disciplined. It is more like a hunch with decent punctuation.

And yet it is now enough to get something moving. Not a fake demo. Not a Figma daydream. Something you can click, react to, dislike, improve.

That still feels significant to me, because it changes where the interesting work starts.

For years, building software meant pushing through a lot of ceremony before you reached the part where taste or product instinct mattered. You had to know the syntax, the tooling, the framework everybody respected this month, and how to unbreak the whole thing when one package manager picked a fight with another.

That all mattered because code was the bottleneck. If you could write it, you had leverage. If you could not, a decent idea often stayed trapped as a note in your phone, a half-finished mockup, or a Notion page called "Concepts" that never recovered.

That gap is narrowing quickly.

So the question changes. If code is no longer the scarcest part of making software, what is?

I keep coming back to the same answer: imagination.

And I do not mean that in the floaty sense. I mean vision. The ability to notice a gap before it has a neat label. To sense that some awful internal workflow, some spreadsheet ritual, or some bit of tolerated friction could be made far better. To picture a product that does not exist yet and describe it clearly enough that it can be built.

Often the raw material is not glamorous at all. Two people in operations are exporting CSVs every Friday, cleaning them by hand, and emailing finance because none of the systems talk to each other properly. For years, the person who noticed that pattern still had a long road ahead before they could test a better version. Now they can often get to a rough first draft quickly enough to find out whether the instinct was right.

That part has always mattered. It was just easier to overlook when so much prestige sat downstream in implementation.

## We Have Probably Been Valuing the Wrong Bit

For a long time, the industry talked as if implementation was the only serious part of the job and everything upstream of it was slightly fluffy. Ideas were cheap. Taste was subjective. Vision was for keynote speakers. The real work, apparently, started when someone opened the editor.

I have never thought that was the full story.

Anyone who has made anything decent knows the invisible part is often the hard part. Knowing what should exist. Knowing what should be left out. Sensing when something technically works but still feels dead on arrival. Knowing the difference between a product that removes friction and one that merely adds another dashboard to somebody's Tuesday.

That is not fluff. That is judgement.

AI makes this more obvious, not less. Once a machine can produce a plausible first draft quickly, more value shifts to the person directing it. Can you tell the difference between competent and compelling? Can you look at something that is almost there and identify the missing piece? Can you resist shipping the first shiny object that sort of works?

That is where more of the leverage is moving. The important shift is not that machines suddenly have better ideas. It is that the cost of turning an idea into a testable first draft has dropped sharply.

## Vibe Coding, Minus the Nonsense

"Vibe coding" is one of those phrases that sounds unserious enough to annoy exactly the people it was always going to annoy. Fair enough. There is a silly version of it online: prompt, generate, post screenshot, declare victory, quietly ignore the fact that the thing collapses the second a real user touches it.

That version deserves the mockery.

But there is a better version, and it is much more interesting than the caricature.

The good version feels less like cheating and more like direction. You start with an instinct: maybe a startup idea, maybe a product hunch, maybe just the feeling that a bit of tolerated nonsense could be removed. You give the machine some boundaries, references, and examples of what good should feel like. Then you react. You tighten. You delete. You ask for another pass. You simplify the layout. You sharpen the language. You realise the first idea was actually three products wearing a trench coat. You cut two of them.

That is messy in the way worthwhile work is usually messy. It is not "press button, receive app." It is iteration, editing, and judgement.

AI reduces the cost of first drafts. It does not remove the need for product judgement, technical judgement, or the boring grown-up work required to make something dependable.

People sometimes talk as if AI removes authorship, but I think it does the opposite. When the machine can generate endlessly, your decisions matter more. It can offer options. It cannot care. It cannot have taste on your behalf. It cannot know what you mean by "make this feel generous" or "make this less smug" unless you can recognise those qualities yourself.

That is not an incidental skill. It is increasingly the work.

## More People Get to Start

The most interesting change here is not pure speed, even though speed gets all the headlines because it looks good in a demo.

The bigger shift is access.

More people can now take part in product discovery, prototyping, and validation without needing a full team before they are allowed to learn anything. A designer can move beyond static screens. A teacher can make a scrappy classroom tool. A parent can automate some repetitive household admin instead of merely resenting it every Sunday evening. A founder can test whether the gap they keep talking about is real before turning it into a personality.

That seems healthy to me.

It does not mean everyone suddenly becomes a software engineer. It means more people can get over the starting line. And once that happens, software starts to feel a little more like literacy and a little less like priesthood.

## Taste Starts Mattering More, Not Less

There is a catch, of course. When making gets easier, curation gets harder.

We are going to drown in average output. In many ways we already are. Landing pages with the same inflated tone. Apps with perfectly decent structure and no pulse. Products that are polished enough to ship and forgettable enough to deserve it.

That is why taste matters so much.

Taste is what stops you accepting the first thing the machine hands back. It is what makes you notice when the copy sounds hollow, when the layout looks expensive but says nothing, when the feature list keeps growing because nobody had the nerve to decide what the product is actually for. It is restraint as much as flair. It is knowing when to make something stranger, when to make it simpler, and when to kill it entirely.

The same applies to code. AI agents are useful, but they are not oracles. They are closer to capable collaborators with uneven judgement. One is quick but slapdash. Another is careful but rigid. Another will make ten sensible choices and then one baffling one with complete confidence, which is honestly a very human flaw.

The skill is less "summon genius machine" and more "become a better editor, reviewer, and product thinker."

## Lowering the Barrier Should Not Mean Lowering the Standard

Plenty of AI-assisted work is still bad. Some of it is lazy. Some of it is careless. Some of it is the same mediocre software as before, just produced faster and marketed harder.

None of this automatically makes the output good.

You still have to think. You still have to test. You still have to notice when the flow is annoying, when the words are clumsy, when the logic fails, or when a feature should be removed rather than expanded. You still have to ask the rude but necessary question: if this vanished tomorrow, would anybody actually miss it?

Lowering the cost of exploration is good. Lowering the cost of delusion is not.

That is the trade I find compelling here: less energy spent wrestling syntax for its own sake, more energy spent deciding what deserves to exist.

## A Practical Filter for Builders

If you are using AI to build, a simple three-part filter helps keep the work grounded:

1. **Is the problem real?**  
   Can you point to a repeated frustration, wasted hour, or awkward workaround that people already feel?
2. **Is the first version useful in one sitting?**  
   If someone uses it for ten minutes, do they leave with less friction than they started with?
3. **Did quality improve with each iteration?**  
   Not just more features. Better clarity, better flow, fewer sharp edges, and fewer assumptions hiding in the logic.

If all three are true, keep going. If not, do not add more scaffolding. Tighten the idea first.

That is the discipline I think this era asks for: prototype faster, yes, but judge harder.

## Final Thought

The future is not going to be short of generated output. We will have more than enough of that.

What will still be rare is original direction. Clear taste. Good judgement. The ability to spot what is missing, see the opening early, and keep pushing until the thing in front of you matches the thing you sensed was possible.

That is the scarce bit now.

At its best, vibe coding gives more people access to that loop. Not because the machine replaces the maker, but because it lowers the wall between intention and expression.

My judgment is simple: that is a meaningful shift, and probably a healthy one, so long as we remember that easier making should raise the bar for judgement rather than lower it.
